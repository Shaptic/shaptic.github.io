Here’s something I’d never be able to put on Twitter because it’s not a 280-character zinger: How proof of immunization to attend school is different than a vaccine mandate for the nation. Most of the required school vaccinations are…

  • Not mandated by the federal government: Each state sets their bar individually; for this rebuttal, I rely on California’s K-12 requirements from here.

  • Given to you as a baby: The required immunizations are typically (or, at least the initial doses are) performed before a child turns two years old.

  • Have been vetted for decades: Polio was authorized a year after its field trials; those started with 1.8m children, while the Moderna vaccine had 600 people in its ongoing Phase 2 trial.

  • Target vulnerable groups: Obviously, you could make the argument that by vaccinating children, we’re protecting them from these diseases for life, but the diseases in question1 are particularly risky for kids:

    • measles is “dangerous, especially for babies and young children” (CDC)
    • whooping cough “can be extremely serious in babies and young children” (CDC)
    • chronic hepatitis B is more likely the younger you are (CDC)
    • chicken pox is an exception: catching it is serious for most of the populaton

  • Encountered resistance at the time, too: While the MMR -> autism link was nonsense in retrospect, it absolutely made sense to be concerned at the time the paper was published. Dissenters existed for most vaccines over the past 150 years, so it shouldn’t be surprising.

In other words, these instances are so different that it makes absolutely no sense to relate them whatsoever. Stop doing it.

While the vaccine may be safe and effective, a nationwide vaccine mandate is an absurd overreach of government authority, period. If you disagree, you like government authority.

  1. I left chicken pox off of this list because the CDC is particularly unhelpful with its analysis. It claims “complications are not common in healthy people,” then immediately turns around to say “people who may be at high risk for complications include: infants, adolescents, adults” which is… basically everybody. I’m unsure about how loosely to interpret the “may”, which is unfortunate because it seems like the best “population severity” comparison to the COVID vaccine mandate. ↩︎
