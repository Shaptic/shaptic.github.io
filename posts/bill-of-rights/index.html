The events that have transpired over the last few months—from the COVID-19 lockdowns to the George Floyd protests—have hopefully opened the eyes of many to the possibility that freedom in our seemingly-democratic nation is merely an illusion that only exists for as long as it’s convenient.

On the Bill of Rights

In this post, I want to tear apart the Constitutional protections “guaranteed” to us by the Founding Fathers one by one with clear evidence outlining how they are ceremonial at best. My fundamental thesis is that the core amendments—our Bill of Rights—are only upheld for the theatrics of democracy: they present a comforting façade that crumbles as soon as it’s convenient for the government to ignore them.

The upside, of course, is that we get to enjoy said rights. Don’t get me wrong: I’m very happy to be able to speak freely, buy a gun, and get a lawyer. The goal of this post is mostly to highlight that those rights are never, by any stretch of the imagination, “inalienable”.

Amendment I

The full text of the First Amendment is as follows:

Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the government for a redress of grievances.

Even in 18th century English, the meaning is pretty clear: the rights to peaceful assembly shall not be infringed. What do you call, then, this 30 minute compilation of police beating non-violent1 protestors? An isolated incident? How about when this march in 2014 led to students being beaten? Just a recent phenomenon? Nope, it’s been happening since at least the 60s. Purely a product of racism, then? Not exactly, since we can see the same things happening during the Occupy movement. I could go on and on… Fundamentally, if you are assembling to advocate for meaningful change, you will be shut down if you push the wrong buttons.

Surely the freedom of the press is safe, then? Totally, as long as you ignore police beating, hacking, and/or arresting journalists as they see fit. And the fact that one corporation controls 40% of the local news market. And that the CIA actively subverts free media expression. And the fact that another two corporations dominate remaining chunk of “free” journalistic expression. Obviously more-independent media exists, but it’s undeniable that a handful of networks provide information to a large-enough portion of the populace that it can influence “democratic” outcomes. To me, that just sounds like state-run propaganda with extra steps.2 The problem is pervasive goes all the way to the top: journalists in charge of keeping our leaders in check can arbitrarily have their press passes revoked.

One could argue about the relative importance of each amendment, but regardless of the ordering, the dissolution of every facet of the First Amendment does not bode well for the idea that our rights have any sort of legal guarantee when facing pressure. Onward…

Amendment II

A well-regulated militia, being necessary to the security of a free state, the right of the people to keep and bear arms, shall not be infringed.

I’ll gloss over the fact that a well-regulated militia doesn’t exist (some argue that the National Guard fulfills this role) and focus on the right to bear arms. I don’t want to go all NRA in this section (though note their ironic silence during the current protests), but it should be obvious to anyone that the current state of firearm ownership is far and away from the intent of the amendment. Whether that’s a good thing or not is a different story; the point still stands. Let’s not forget that the rich employ private security to keep them safe; these forces are equipped far better than even the most die-hard 2nd Amendment fan. Isn’t this yet another case in which the “regular” rules don’t apply to the wealthy?

Amendment IV

Most of the subsequent amendments aim to protect our legal rights in a criminal context:

The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.

In simpler terms, probable cause is required to enact searches that would otherwise be considered unreasonable. And yet, the stop-and-frisk policy in NYC is almost undeniably the complete antithesis of probable cause, and police literally plant drugs on people to manufacture “cause.”

Surveillance is constitutionally considered a form of search, and yet, the NSA indiscriminately watches all of the Internet traffic in the country and continually builds up a suite of hacking tools, as the pivotal Snowden revelations showed us. Law enforcement performs warrant-less wiretaps on cell phones via Stingrays and has an arsenal of other mass surveillance tools that don’t require a second thought from a legal perspective. And let’s not even talk about how the PATRIOT Act essentially made judges and warrants unnecessary if the FBI wants to perform extensive surveillance and data gathering on citizens.

In theory, shouldn’t this firehose of information make it absurdly easy for law enforcement officers (LEOs) to prevent crimes and apprehend criminals? Au contraire, mon frère. Unless, of course, the state itself is under attack: then, the government will leverage its arsenal of tools and knowledge to its full potential.

Amendment V

No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a grand jury, except in cases arising in the land or naval forces, or in the militia, when in actual service in time of war or public danger; nor shall any person be subject for the same offense to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb; nor shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself, nor be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law; nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation.

Colloquially, this preserves our right to due process. Crucially, a person must not be deprived of life, liberty, and property throughout it. And yet, the police are allowed to seize property even in the absence of a crime. In fact, we (the people) lose more stuff to civil asset forfeiture than to burglaries.

Deprivation of liberty is discussed in the next section, and I don’t even want to talk about being deprived of life… the statistics speak for themselves. And let’s not forget about government programs that preyed upon “people who could not fight back” to run experiments on them. These were completely innocent people. Probable cause? Due process? I hardly know her…

The caveat in the amendment about times of war is doubly-interesting: despite the fact that Congress holds the exclusive right to declare war, it hasn’t done so since 1942. Last I checked, though, we’ve been at war 90% of the time since then (Vietnam, Korea, Middle East, etc.)…

Amendment VI

In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right to a speedy and public trial, by an impartial jury of the state and district wherein the crime shall have been committed, which district shall have been previously ascertained by law, and to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation; to be confronted with the witnesses against him; to have compulsory process for obtaining witnesses in his favor, and to have the assistance of counsel for his defense.

Wouldn’t it be nice if this amendment was upheld? Apparently we defined “speedy” in 1974 as being… over three months? Nice. That aside, we still have people stuck in jail for 35 years without a trial and thousands of others waiting more than two years. In fact, something like 20% of prisoners are awaiting trials and possible convinctions. That’s just… absurd. Of course, it becomes apparent why this is the case when you consider that many prisons are privately-owned, and that the 13th Amendment literally lets prisoners be treated like slaves, but that’s a whole separate can of worms. We even have an entire area off-shore dedicated to imprisoning people (including U.S. citizens) without a trial.

Amendment VIII

Excessive bail shall not be required, nor excessive fines imposed, nor cruel and unusual punishments inflicted.

The subjectivity of this amendment is, of course, laughable, and it leads directly to obvious violations.

I mean, I guess if you ignore waterboarding—ahem, sorry, “enhanced interrogation techniques”; prison sentences for drug possession (up to 10 years) relative to, say, sexual assault of an unconscious student (6 months); or thousand-dollar fines for driving without insurance (because fining someone who can’t afford insurance makes sense) then sure, the government has never inflicted cruel and unusual punishment upon people or fined them excessively.

In Conclusion…

The United States has truly evolved into a perfect system for a select, privileged few. The revolving door between corporations, international banks, and the federal government means the same groups are pulling the strings. The beautiful promises of democracy and first-world comforts keep us happy and our good nature makes us wish those luxuries for others. We’ve bought into the lie that everyone can live like we do as those same promises are used to stage coups, destroy ecosystems, and funnel wealth back to the same groups at the top.

The “economic hitman” john something put it best:

— Confessions of an Economic Hitman

And if we try to change any of that, and I mean really change it, we’re met with physical, economic, and legal resistance on all fronts. Our “rights” seem to wither away at the most opportune moments. I think there are only two ways to move forward if you acknowledge this truth:

  • There’s nothing we can do about it; the simulation will end soon anyway.
