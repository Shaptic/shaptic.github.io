Outreach Thoughts
A necessary word-vomit of thoughts on the homeless outreach event I participated in on January 11th, 2020… to become a little more structured soon.
Jan 11, 2020
 · 
777 words
 · 
4 minute read
· #diary

How? How can God allow this to happen? Yet it’s not fair to blame God. Humans are the ones that allow this to happen. As they said in the final prayer, we as humans have to choose to serve God and do His will through our own hands. We can’t expect Him to magically whip up a solution and solve the homeless crisis… (which isn’t to say that He couldn’t, but we cannot blame him for respecting our free will and not interfering in our lives directly… we are reaping what we’ve sown). At the end of the day, it’s up to us.

Then the question transforms: How can WE allow this to happen? The richest country in the world, yet an entire underground society, with its own rules, hierarchies, and customs, sprouts in (probably) every city and somehow flourishes (if you can call it that).

What have we done?

Gratitude. I’ve never seen such joy for such (what we consider to be) trifles. The less we have, the more we’re thankful for. The more we have, the less meaningful a gift is, especially if we can afford that gift for ourselves. If you don’t want for much, new things mean less. If you need, I mean truly need, then to receive the gifts you need begets unparalleled gratitude. It’s almost, actually not even almost, it really is genuinely uncomfortable. I didn’t deserve any of the good thoughts that were sent my way. I was doing something so simple, so elementary, so within my means that it was almost farcical to be praised for it. Is doing good deeds in secret better? Does hearing “thank you” actually make the experience worse, because you know you don’t deserve the thanks? Or is it the very fact that you feel that you don’t deserve it the entire point… You shouldn’t get to run away from the feelings that this morning came with.

Honestly, the average person I encountered on this special morning seemed more content with their life than the average person I encounter in the day-to-day. Despite their struggles and difficulties and needs, they wore their heart on their sleeve and nothing was hidden. Their feelings and thoughts were all in plain sight. Whether the hug for the boots, the earnest questions about what we were offering (almost aggressively inquiring about food, or a second tarp, or a bag, etc.), the fact that some completely ignored us, it was all right there in the open. There were no games, no hiding or malicious intent (you can hardly call taking everything that’s offered you malicious behaviour or greed), nothing beyond a pure soul-to-soul open line of communication that was only inhibited by my own discomfort and unfamiliarity.

Out of sight, out of mind.

Maslow was right.

Ignorance is bliss, but I’m glad I know.

“Forgetting is as important as remembering,” but this experience definitely falls into the latter category.

My God, seeing those kids…

I still can’t conceive or wrap my head around the fact that that goes on every single day and I was that overwhelmed by a small, insignificant taste of their life that I CHOSE to partake in. Many of them did not choose, or no longer realize that they had (or have) a choice. I was a tourist in their life and evaporated back into civilization as soon as I could escape it.

I don’t know what to do with the experience.

Do I do it again?

Do I try to integrate what I felt in my day-to-day, and continue being completely dissociated from what are now obviously trifles of the day-to-day?

Do I just try to be more open and compassionate towards the less-fortunate? Should I start carrying a blanket, some gloves, and a bottle of water in my car so that I can always help someone in need?

Honestly the scene near the Loaves & Fishes felt like something straight out of an apocalyptic novel. The crowd of disheveled people carrying their scraps protectedly, the clear existence of a social structure yet the fact that its internals were hidden from view, the families keeping their flock together and suffering through the circumstances as a team, the complete lack of shame in their conditions…

I have never felt such a foreign, indescribable potpourri of feelings.

  • How and why is this happening?
  • Why are we the only ones out here?
  • I’m not doing enough.
  • Is this real?
  • The good feelings were short-lived.
  • Utterly defeating and overwhelming sorrow.
  • Does it end?
  • Brief pockets of joy when someone accepted something–why? dig deeper
  • Discomfort from any appreciation.
  • Is our impact non-zero?
  • Awkwardness when ignored.
  • Fear.
  • Shock.
  • Despair.

Sandy
